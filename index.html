Find and use connections between nutrients

The Cedar Nexus project is the synthesis of two initiatives I am currently working on. It's a long-term effort to understand how nutrients interact with each other and how this knowledge can be applied to create a more sustainable and healthy food system. The core idea is to identify the most important connections between nutrients and to develop a method for predicting and optimizing these interactions. The project is still in its early stages, but I am excited to see where it will take me.

Nutrideep - A Nutrient Prediction Tool

Predict Missing Nutrients

By leveraging deep learning techniques, I have developed a tool that predicts missing nutrients from a given set of known nutrients. For example, if you know the energy, protein, fat, and sugar content of a food item, the model can predict the levels of missing nutrients such as vitamins, minerals, and amino acids with remarkable accuracy.

The predictive model analyzes known data to fill in the gaps in nutritional information, providing a much more comprehensive profile of a food item. This helps users understand not just what they eat, but how it contributes to their overall nutritional needs.

How it works

  • Input: Nutrient data (e.g., calories, protein, fat, sugar, salt) from food packaging.
  • Prediction: The machine learning model predicts missing nutrient values, such as specific vitamins or amino acids.
  • Output: A fuller, more accurate nutritional profile of the food item, including predicted values.

The model's high accuracy (with an R of 0.97 for predicting amino acid content) makes it a valuable tool for anyone seeking a more complete understanding of their nutritional intake.

How it performs

The following diagram shows the predicted values tested against actual values for the amino acid serine. The high accuracy of the model can be seen by the clustering of data points around the 1:1 line, indicating that the model is able to accurately predict the values of serine up to 20g per 100g of food.

The model's ability to predict values across a wide range of concentrations is important, as it means that it can be used to accurately predict the nutrient content of a wide variety of foods.

As with any machine learning model, there is always room for improvement. There are still some outliers that the model struggles to predict accurately. These discrepancies highlight areas for further refinement and optimization of the model. By continuously updating and validating the model with new data and refining its predictions, its performance can be enhanced, ensuring more reliable predictions across diverse datasets.

In addition to amino acids, the model also predicts the nutritional values of vitamins and minerals, which can be useful for food consumers and health professionals.

Nutrimerge - A Network of Nutrient Interactions

Focus on dynamic interactions

Just as a tree is much more than its roots, branches and leaves, the nutrients you digest are not just the sum of what you eat. Dietary fiber, proteins, carbohydrates, fats and various micronutrients interact with each other and impact absorption to a varying degree.

Traditional approaches simply add together the ingredients of the food consumed. In terms of calories, that would mean 575 Kcal in a simple example of an avocado and some broccoli.

= 575 Kcal

However, this is not what happens in real life, where emergent processes from dynamic interactions of the food matrix play an important role. My approach tries to cover as many known interactions as possible in this way:

= 430 Kcal

Just looking at the interactions between the four nutrients - fibre, fat, protein and calcium - makes a significant difference and results in 430 instead of 575 Kcal in this example.

The maths

To begin with, I have designed a serial bus system, where the calculations for each interaction are carried out one after the other. Each calculation is performed using the results of the previous calculation.
These calculations are based on the averages of many study results, reconstructed into mathematical functions. For very complex interactions, piecewise functions are used.

The following diagram tentatively shows how the intake of total energy, protein and fat is affected by the amount of fibre consumed, based on the results of various studies (Wisker et al., 1988, Baer et al., 1997, Corbin et al., 2023).

Another illustrative example is the phenomenon that calcium undergoes a saponification reaction with fat in the intestine, the result being a poorly soluble reaction product that is excreted undigested.
